## Why sample stages get confused

In apparel development, the word “sample” is used loosely. Buyers ask for a sample, factories send a sample, and both sides assume they are talking about the same checkpoint. Often they are not. Two of the most commonly mixed stages are fit samples and pre-production samples (PPS). Understanding the difference protects timelines, budgets, and production quality.

This article explains what each stage is for, what it should include, and how Canadian buyers can use both without unnecessary delay.

## What a fit sample is for

A fit sample is primarily about shape, proportion, and wearability. It answers questions such as:

- Does the silhouette match the intended design?
- Are key measurements workable on the body?
- Do sleeve length, rise, shoulder slope, and balance feel right?
- Are major construction choices compatible with the fit goal?

Fit samples may use available fabrics that approximate the bulk material when the exact fabric is not yet ready. That approximation is useful for pattern decisions—but it is also a limitation. A fit sample made from substitute fabric can validate proportions while still leaving fabric behaviour questions open.

## What a pre-production sample is for

A pre-production sample is the commercial reference for bulk. Ideally, it represents:

- Approved bulk or near-bulk fabric and shade
- Final or near-final trims and components
- Agreed construction methods
- Decoration executed to approved artwork and placement
- Labelling and finishing close to shipment standards

When a buyer approves a PPS, they are saying: produce like this. That is why PPS approval is such a consequential checkpoint. Changes after PPS often mean cost, timeline, or quality risk.

## Side-by-side comparison

| Topic | Fit sample | Pre-production sample |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Primary purpose | Validate pattern and wearability | Lock the bulk production reference |
| Fabric | May be substitute or development fabric | Should match approved bulk fabric where possible |
| Colour | May be temporary | Should reflect approved shade standards |
| Decoration | Optional or approximate | Should match approved method and placement |
| Decision outcome | Revise pattern and measurements | Release or hold production |

Both stages matter. Skipping fit and jumping straight to PPS can waste time if major pattern issues appear late. Approving fit and skipping PPS can allow fabric, trim, or decoration differences into bulk unnoticed.

## When buyers should request each stage

Request a **fit sample** when:

- You are developing a new style or fit block
- Measurements have changed significantly from a previous order
- The category is fit-sensitive (woven shirts, trousers, tailored looks, structured uniforms)
- Previous bulk had recurring fit complaints

Request a **pre-production sample** when:

- Fabric, colour, trims, and decoration are substantially ready
- You need a reference before cutting bulk
- The order is commercially meaningful enough that ambiguity is expensive
- Multiple factories or seasons need a shared standard

Some programs also use proto samples earlier and size-set samples between fit and PPS. The exact sequence should match category risk—not habit.

## Common process mistakes

### Treating any sample as PPS

A nicely sewn garment in the wrong fabric is not a bulk reference. If shade, hand feel, stretch, or shrinkage will differ in bulk, the fit sample should not be treated as final approval.

### Approving fit verbally and forgetting the record

Fit comments should be written against points of measure. “Make it a bit longer” is not a production instruction. Revised measurement charts and dated comments are.

### Changing artwork after PPS approval

Decoration changes after PPS can invalidate the approved reference. If artwork must change, decide whether a new decorated sample or strike-off is required before bulk continues.

### Running parallel changes without control

Buyers sometimes revise fit, fabric, and decoration at the same time. That can be necessary—but only with clear version control. Otherwise factories may combine an old pattern with a new fabric and a newer logo placement.

## How sampling comments should be structured

Effective comments are specific:

- Point of measure and required change
- Construction note (for example, seam type or reinforcement)
- Fabric or shade observation
- Decoration placement or quality note
- Packaging or label note if relevant at that stage

Separate fit comments from bulk-readiness comments. A fit round should not become an informal dumping ground for every future commercial decision.

## Implications for lead time

Each sample stage consumes calendar time: cutting, sewing, finishing, shipping to Canada if physical review is required, buyer evaluation, and factory revision. Buyers who compress sampling without reducing decision quality usually transfer risk into production.

A practical approach is to decide early:

- Which styles need full fit development
- Which carry-over styles can move faster from known blocks
- Whether reviews will be physical, photo-based, or a combination
- Who has authority to approve each stage

Clear ownership reduces idle time between factory readiness and buyer response.

## How this affects costing

Fit development may involve pattern work and multiple revisions. PPS may involve bulk materials and decoration setup. Buyers should ask what sampling fees apply and whether revision rounds are included. Transparent assumptions prevent later commercial friction.

## Practical recommendation for Canadian buyers

Use fit samples to settle the body and pattern. Use pre-production samples to settle the commercial product. Do not ask one sample to do both jobs unless the program is simple, materials are confirmed, and risk is low.

If you are unsure which stage you are reviewing, ask: *If I approve this, can the factory cut bulk tomorrow?* If the answer is no—because fabric, shade, trims, or decoration are unfinished—you are not looking at a true PPS.

## Closing guidance

Fit and PPS are complementary controls. Fit protects wearability. PPS protects production fidelity. Together, they reduce the chance that Canadian buyers discover problems only after goods are packed for export.
