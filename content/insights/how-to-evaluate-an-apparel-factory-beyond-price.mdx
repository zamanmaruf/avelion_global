## Price is information—not a decision

Unit price matters. It always will. But for Canadian buyers sourcing apparel from Asia, the lowest quoted price is rarely the full story. A factory that underprices relative to the true specification may later recover margin through construction shortcuts, delayed materials, weak finishing, or endless clarification cycles.

Evaluating a manufacturer beyond price does not mean ignoring cost. It means reading cost in context: capability, capacity, quality systems, communication, and commercial transparency.

## Start with category capability

Ask whether the factory regularly produces garments like yours. Relevant signals include:

- Product category experience (knits, wovens, outerwear, uniforms, workwear)
- Familiarity with your fabric type and GSM range
- Machinery and process relevance to the construction
- Decoration capability in-house or through controlled partners
- Examples of comparable complexity—without treating every brochure claim as proof

A factory optimized for basic jersey tees may struggle with structured woven shirts or reinforced workwear. Category mismatch is one of the most expensive forms of “cheap.”

## Review capacity against your calendar

Capability without capacity creates late deliveries. Discuss:

- Current and projected loading for your production window
- Minimum order expectations by style and colour
- Flexibility for staggered shipments if needed
- Backup planning for critical programs

Buyers should be cautious of factories that accept every order enthusiastically without discussing line plans. Capacity honesty is a trust signal.

## Examine sampling discipline

Strong factories treat sampling as a controlled process. Look for:

- Clear sample stage definitions
- Written revision tracking
- Realistic sample lead times
- Willingness to raise missing specification details early

Factories that ask precise questions during sampling are often easier to manage in bulk than factories that stay quiet and interpret freely.

## Assess quality systems without overclaiming

Quality evaluation should be practical:

- How are measurements checked during production?
- What inline and final inspection practices are used?
- How are defects classified and reported?
- How are shade continuity and fabric issues handled?
- What retained references are kept for reorders?

Be careful with certification language. Certificates and audit reports can be useful inputs for buyer review, but they do not automatically guarantee garment quality for your style. ## Test communication quality early

Communication problems rarely improve after the purchase order. Evaluate:

- Response clarity and turnaround during quotation
- Whether assumptions are stated or hidden
- How the factory handles conflicting instructions
- Who owns technical questions versus commercial questions
- How status is reported during production

For Canadian buyers, time-zone coverage matters. A Canada-based sourcing partner can keep communication moving while factory follow-through continues with Asian manufacturing partners.

## Read the quotation like an auditor

Beyond the unit price, examine:

- Fabric and trim assumptions
- Decoration inclusions
- Packaging standards
- Sampling costs and revision policies
- Payment terms and Incoterms under discussion
- Lead-time language and dependency notes

Two quotations are comparable only when assumptions are comparable. If one quote excludes packaging, labels, or decoration setup that another includes, the cheaper number may simply be incomplete.

## Consider commercial posture and relationship fit

Not every capable factory is the right partner for every buyer. Consider:

- Willingness to support reorders and style continuity
- Openness about subcontracting when buyers require disclosure
- Comfort with your documentation expectations
- Cultural fit around problem escalation—early honesty versus late surprise

Long-term programs benefit from factories that value process as much as volume.

## Use a structured scorecard

A simple evaluation scorecard might weight:

1. Category capability
2. Capacity and lead-time realism
3. Sampling and technical clarity
4. Quality process maturity
5. Communication reliability
6. Documentation readiness
7. Commercial transparency
8. Price competitiveness

Price remains on the scorecard—just not alone at the top.

## Red flags worth pausing for

Pause and investigate when you see:

- Reluctance to explain quotation assumptions
- Vague answers about where production will occur
- Pressure to skip pre-production approval
- Unrealistic lead times relative to material readiness
- Resistance to measurement charts or written comments
- Dramatic price gaps without a technical explanation

None of these automatically disqualify a factory, but each deserves a direct conversation before commitment.

## How Canadian buyers can apply this

Before awarding an order, consolidate your evaluation into a short recommendation note:

- Why this factory fits the category
- What risks remain and how they will be monitored
- Which sample stages are mandatory
- What quality checkpoints will apply
- What commercial assumptions are locked

That note becomes useful later if teams change or if a reorder needs historical context.

## Closing guidance

The goal is not to find a perfect factory. The goal is to choose a suitable factory with open eyes, then manage the relationship with clear specifications and checkpoints. Price tells you what you might pay. Evaluation tells you what you are likely to receive.
