## Why a checklist beats a brochure

Canadian buyers evaluating Asian manufacturing partners often receive capability decks, price lists, and confident timelines. Those materials are useful starting points. They are not due diligence.

Avelion’s operating model treats Asia as the manufacturing umbrella and Bangladesh as our primary manufacturing market. The checklist below is written for that reality: category fit, capacity honesty, documentation posture, and Canada-bound delivery readiness—not a generic “find any factory” hunt.

Use this as a planning aid before sampling spend accelerates. It is not a certification claim, audit substitute, or guarantee that every factory will meet every item.

## 1. Confirm category capability

Ask whether the manufacturer regularly produces garments like yours:

- Product family (knits, wovens, uniforms, workwear, outerwear, decorated apparel)
- Fabric type and approximate GSM range
- Construction complexity relative to their core lines
- Decoration pathway (in-house or controlled partner)
- Comparable programs—without treating every marketing claim as proof

Category mismatch is one of the most expensive forms of “cheap.” A jersey specialist and a structured woven house are not interchangeable because both can sew a polo.

## 2. Test capacity against your calendar

Capability without capacity creates late deliveries. Discuss:

- Loading for your intended production window
- MOQ posture by style and colour
- Flexibility for staged shipments if your Canadian receiving plan requires it
- How they handle overlapping peak seasons

Treat enthusiastic acceptance of every volume band without line-plan discussion as a risk signal, not a win.

## 3. Review sampling and approval discipline

Before bulk talk, understand how samples are managed:

- Which sample types they expect for your category
- How measurement charts and construction notes are controlled
- Whether approvals are written and retained as the production reference
- Typical revision cycles when inputs are incomplete

Sampling is where ambiguity becomes expensive. Factories that rush to bulk without locking references usually create rework later.

## 4. Inspect commercial transparency

Quotations should be comparable. Ask for clarity on:

- Fabric, trim, and decoration assumptions
- Packing, labelling, and carton expectations
- What is included versus excluded under the discussed Incoterms
- Validity period and change triggers

Opaque commercial assumptions make “lowest price” meaningless. Transparent assumptions make negotiation useful.

## 5. Check quality and documentation posture

For programs shipping into Canada, documentation and quality systems matter early:

- How inline and final checks are planned against an approved sample
- Measurement and workmanship control expectations
- Document types they can typically support (factory, testing, origin, packing)
- Response process when clarification is required

Document availability depends on the selected manufacturer, product, and buyer requirements. Do not treat a checklist as a promise that every certificate exists on demand.

## 6. Align logistics handoff expectations

Even strong production fails if shipment readiness is improvised:

- Who prepares commercial invoice and packing list drafts
- Carton marking standards for Canadian consignees
- Freight-forwarder handoff timing
- Broker liaison expectations (Avelion coordinates; licensed brokers clear)

Delivery into Canada is part of the commercial outcome. Plan it before cargo is waiting on the dock.

## 7. Decide what “good enough to shortlist” means

A shortlist should answer:

- Can this factory make *this* product?
- Can they make it in *this* window at a realistic MOQ?
- Are commercial assumptions comparable across options?
- Is there a clear path from sample approval to bulk and Canadian delivery?

If those answers are still vague, keep evaluating—or pause sampling spend until the brief is clearer.
