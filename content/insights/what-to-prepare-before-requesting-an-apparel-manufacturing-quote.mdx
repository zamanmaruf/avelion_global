## Better inputs create better quotations

When Canadian buyers request apparel manufacturing quotes with incomplete information, factories fill gaps with assumptions. Those assumptions may be reasonable—or commercially convenient. Either way, the resulting numbers are hard to compare and easy to misunderstand.

Preparing a clear request for quotation (RFQ) does not slow sourcing down. It usually speeds it up by reducing clarification cycles and false comparisons. This guide outlines what to prepare before you ask for pricing.

## 1. Define the product clearly

Include enough detail for a factory to visualize the garment:

- Product category and silhouette
- Front/back references or tech-pack sketches
- Target construction level (basic, mid, elevated)
- Intended end use (retail, uniform, promotional, private label)
- Gender or fit block expectations

If you have a physical reference sample, say whether factories should match it closely or use it only as inspiration. Those are different costing problems.

## 2. Share quantity bands that reflect reality

Factories need volume context. Provide:

- Estimated total units
- Breakdown by style, colour, and size if known
- Whether the order is a one-time run or a recurring program
- Any phased delivery expectations

Exact purchase-order quantities can come later. Honest bands are more useful than inflated optimism or vague “TBD” notes.

## 3. Specify materials and acceptable alternatives

Material ambiguity is one of the largest quotation variables. Prepare:

- Fibre content targets
- Fabric construction and GSM or weight ranges
- Any required finishes
- Trim expectations for zippers, buttons, elastics, and labels
- Whether buyer-supplied materials are involved

If alternatives are acceptable, define the boundary. “Similar fabric” is not a specification. “Comparable hand feel within an agreed GSM range and cotton-rich content” is closer to usable guidance.

## 4. Clarify colour strategy

Colour affects dyeing, MOQs, and lead times. State:

- Number of colourways
- Whether colours are standards, seasonal, or custom
- Any contrast trim colours
- Whether shade continuity with previous bulk is required

Custom colours often introduce lab-dip steps and dyeing minimums. Buyers should expect those dependencies to appear in both cost and timeline discussions.

## 5. Include decoration and artwork status

Decoration can change cost as much as fabric. Provide:

- Decoration method preferences
- Logo sizes and placements
- Number of print colours or embroidery locations
- Artwork readiness (concept only versus production-ready files)
- Whether samples must include decoration

If artwork is not final, ask for quotation assumptions to be stated clearly so later artwork changes can be re-costed transparently.

## 6. State packaging and presentation requirements

Packaging is frequently omitted and then disputed. Include:

- Folding method
- Polybag and sticker expectations
- Hangtags and brand presentation
- Carton quantity targets
- Assortment packing versus solid size/colour packing
- Any kit-building needs for promotional programs

Factories cannot cost what they have not been told to include.

## 7. Explain timeline expectations carefully

Share:

- Target ex-factory or delivery windows
- Hard deadlines (events, intake dates, academic calendars)
- Flexibility, if any, around partial shipments
- Sample review turnaround capacity on the buyer side

Ask for indicative lead times with dependencies listed—fabric readiness, sample approvals, decoration setup, and shipping mode. Treat fixed-date promises with caution until materials and approvals are confirmed.

## 8. Outline commercial and logistics preferences

Helpful commercial context includes:

- Preferred Incoterms for discussion
- Destination country and provisional delivery point
- Any freight-forwarder or broker already involved
- Documentation your organization typically reviews
- Payment-term expectations for conversation (not as unilateral demands in a first RFQ)

You do not need a full contract to request a quote. You do need enough commercial framing for factories to respond intelligently.

## 9. Identify quality and compliance expectations

State practical expectations such as:

- Required sample stages before bulk
- Inspection expectations
- Testing requirements, if any
- Labelling requirements for your market
- Factory documentation your team reviews

Avoid vague phrases like “full compliance with all international standards” without naming what you actually need. Specific requests produce specific responses.

## 10. Assign internal decision owners

Quotes stall when factories reply and nobody on the buyer side owns the next decision. Before sending an RFQ, confirm:

- Who reviews technical assumptions
- Who compares commercial offers
- Who approves sample budgets
- How quickly your team can respond to clarification questions

A prepared buyer is a faster partner.

## What a strong RFQ package looks like

At minimum, aim to send:

1. Product description and sketches or tech pack
2. Quantity bands
3. Material and colour guidance
4. Decoration details and artwork status
5. Packaging notes
6. Timing windows
7. Destination and commercial preferences for discussion
8. Sample and quality expectations

Even a concise package with those elements outperforms a long email that leaves the important details implied.
