## Sampling is a control system, not a formality

For Canadian buyers manufacturing apparel in Asia—often through Bangladesh as a primary production market—sampling is where commercial intent becomes an operational standard. When approvals are verbal, incomplete, or skipped, bulk production inherits ambiguity. Ambiguity becomes returns, discounts, or missed Canadian intake dates.

Sample approval discipline means treating each relevant sample stage as a written gate: what was reviewed, what was accepted, what must change, and what the factory may proceed against.

## Match sample types to program risk

Not every program needs every sample type. Choose stages based on construction complexity, decoration risk, and reorder importance:

### Prototype / development sample

Used to prove silhouette, construction approach, and material direction when the product is still being defined. Approve directionally, then move to more controlled samples once the brief stabilizes.

### Fit sample

Used to validate measurements, balance, and wearer comfort against the chart and end use. Fit approvals should reference measurement points—not only “looks good.”

### Size-set sample

Used when graded sizes matter for retail, uniforms, or multi-site programs. Size-set discipline reduces bulk surprises across the curve.

### Pre-production sample (PPS)

Used as the commercial reference before bulk accelerates. Fabric, construction, decoration, labelling, and finish should match what you expect to receive—within agreed tolerances.

Skipping PPS on complex or decorated programs is a common source of late conflict. Skipping prototypes on undefined products wastes money differently: you pay for bulk-like samples too early.

## Write the approval—every time

A useful approval record typically includes:

- Sample type and style reference
- Date reviewed and reviewer name/role
- Decision: approved / approved with comments / rejected
- Specific comments tied to measurements, construction, materials, or decoration
- Whether bulk may proceed, and under which conditions

“Looks fine—go ahead” in a chat thread is not a durable production standard. When people change or disputes arise, written records protect both buyer and manufacturer.

## Separate “nice to have” comments from hard stops

Unstructured feedback creates churn. Separate:

- **Hard stops** that must change before the next sample or before bulk
- **Preferences** that are optional or season-two improvements
- **Buyer-owned decisions** still missing (artwork, label copy, packing rules)

Factories cannot execute ambiguous preference lists as if they were engineering requirements. Clarity speeds sampling; ambiguity multiplies rounds.

## Lock the reference before volume commits

Once a pre-production sample is approved, treat it as the production reference unless a written change is agreed. Common failure patterns include:

- Changing fabric or decoration after PPS without resetting commercial assumptions
- Allowing bulk to start while major comments remain open
- Approving photos alone when measurements or hand feel still need physical confirmation

Avelion’s product-development and quality work for Canadian programs emphasizes this lock: sampling should reduce risk, not create a second negotiation during cutting.

## Connect sampling to Canada delivery reality

Sample discipline also protects logistics. Packing methods, fold standards, polybag rules, and carton marks often need confirmation before freight handoff into Canada. If those details only appear after cargo is ready, document and receiving friction increase.

Sampling is not only about the garment on a hanger. It is about the agreed standard that production, inspection, and shipment coordination will follow.

## Practical starting point for buyers

Before requesting samples, prepare:

- Product category and end use
- Measurement chart or clear size intent
- Material and decoration assumptions (or known open questions)
- Labelling and packaging expectations
- Target timeline and destination in Canada

Incomplete briefs are normal. Incomplete approvals are not.
